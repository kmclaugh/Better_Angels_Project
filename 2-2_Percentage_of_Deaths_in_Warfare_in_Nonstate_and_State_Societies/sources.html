Data Notes:

The 3 percent estimate for Europe, 1900-1960 comes from Wright's A Study of War, page 245. The first edition was completed in November 1941, before World War II's most destructive years. The figure was unchanged, however, in the 1965 revision, page 245, and in the 1964 abridgment, page 60, though the latter mentions Dresden, Hiroshima, and Nagasaki in the same paragraph. Pinker assumes the unchanged estimate was intentional, and that the additional world war deaths were offset by the billion people added to the world during the more fecund and less lethal postwar decades.

The world-wide total battle deaths in the 20th Century are summed for the years 1900 through 1945 inclusive from the three Correlates of War datasets (Inter-State, Extrastate, and Intrastate), using the larger figure of the two columns "State Deaths" and "Total Deaths", together with the geometric mean of the "Battle Dead Low" and "Battle Dead High" estimates for the years 1946 to 2000 inclusive from the PRIO Battle Deaths Dataset.

The 6 billion total deaths denominator used in both the World (wars and genocides) 20th Century and World (battle deaths) 20th Century figures comes from John Mueller's estimate that 12 billion people lived in the 20th century and that about 5.75 billion were alive at the century's end.

According to the the Statistical Abstract of the United States, 2,448,017 Americans died in 2005. According to icasualties.org there were 846 American battle deaths in Iraq and 99 in Afghanistan.
